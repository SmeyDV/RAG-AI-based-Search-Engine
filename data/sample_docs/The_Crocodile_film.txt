The Crocodile (Khmer: នេសាទក្រពើ)

Year: 2005
Director: Mao Ayuth
Genre: Horror, Action
Country: Cambodia
Language: Khmer
Runtime: 120 minutes
Budget: $100,000

Cast:
Dy Saveth
Preap Sovath
Sim Solika Rathanak

Plot Summary:
A group of villagers live by a crocodile-infested river which has claimed the lives of many of their relatives. After losing his wife, family members and neighbours to the crocodiles, local farmer San decides to become a crocodile hunter. His aim is to find and kill the Crocodile King, the legendary giant crocodile responsible for terrorizing his village and devouring his loved ones. The film combines elements of natural horror with action sequences as San battles the deadly creature in its river territory.

Production:
The film's budget was US$100,000, a high level for a Cambodian production at the time. A significant amount of this went towards the film's lead actor, Preap Sovath, who was one of Cambodia's most popular singers and performers. The film was written, produced and directed by Mao Ayuth, who was one of the few filmmakers from the golden age of Cambodian cinema to survive the Khmer Rouge regime. The film was first released in July 2005 in six theatres across Cambodia and has periodically returned to Cambodian cinemas. As of 2021, the film has never been released on DVD or streaming platforms, leading to a viral social media campaign using the hashtag "#releaseនេសាទក្រពើ" demanding its digital release.

Awards:
The Crocodile won six awards at the Khmer National Film Celebration, including Best Movie, Best Director and Best Writer for Mao Ayuth, and Best Actor for Preap Sovath.

Reception:
The Crocodile was a major commercial success in Cambodia and is considered one of the most notable Cambodian horror films of the 2000s. It helped establish Preap Sovath as a film star and demonstrated that locally produced genre films could compete with international imports at the Cambodian box office.

Overview:
The Crocodile is a 2005 Cambodian horror-action film directed by Mao Ayuth, starring Preap Sovath and Dy Saveth. With a budget of $100,000, it was one of the most expensive Cambodian productions at the time. The film became a landmark in modern Cambodian cinema and won multiple national film awards.
